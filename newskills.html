Combinators

There are four different combinators in CSS. Let's use "div" as an example:
  • Descendant selector (space) - Selects all elements inside "div"
  • Child selector (>) - Selects all elements that are immediate children of "div"
  • Adjacent sibling selector (+) - Selects the next sibling of "div"
  • General sibling selector (~) - Selects all following siblings of "div"

Framing Pseudo-Elements

Let's say you are managing a website that provides instructional content for your target audience. Let's say it teaches the audience how to do something while also providing important details and context. All that extra info is important, but your primary goal was the how-to function so you want to attract extra attention to the instructions. Add three brief paragraphs here.

The first paragraph should be a step-by-step instruction. The second paragraph should be some additional and supporting information, but not necessary to actually complete the task. The third paragraph will be another part of the how-to instruction. Call attention to those instructions by inserting the beegif image before and after the relevant elements.

First, it is critical to detail out the purpose and goal of your website. Second, you will want to narrow down exactly who your target audience is.

Without knowing who your target audience is, the scope of your design and coding will feel umanageable. You will be trying to please EVERYONE and ultimately pleasing NONE because the content and design will just be far too generic. This is an important step that should not be skipped.

Now that you have the purpose, goal, and target audience defined get out and do some fact checking. Do members of your target audience actually have a need that your website fulfills?
